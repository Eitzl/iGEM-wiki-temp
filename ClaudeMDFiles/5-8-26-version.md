***AMPs: the future of antibiotics***

**Why AMPs Matter**

Antimicrobial peptides represent a promising alternative to traditional antibiotics—and we need them urgently. As bacteria develop resistance to conventional drugs, AMPs offer a different mechanism: they typically disrupt bacterial membranes or interfere with vital processes in ways that are harder for pathogens to evolve around. What makes them particularly compelling for biotech is that they're naturally occurring (found in everything from frog skin to human immune cells), so we can engineer them, optimize them, and deploy them faster. They're smaller than full proteins, more specific than broad-spectrum drugs, and could help us fight infections that are becoming untreatable by conventional means—which is both a public health crisis and a massive opportunity for the companies that crack therapeutic delivery.

The specific target: Lyme disease

The 2026 UC Santa Cruz iGEM team is a group of 20 undergraduate students in the Biomolecular Engineering & Bioinformatics (BMEB) department at UC Santa Cruz. We are collaboratively designing and building a synthetic biology project as part of the international [iGEM](https://igem.org/) program. Our team is united by the goal of applying science and engineering to serve underserved communities.  
Our project was inspired by reports of Lyme disease patients self-administering bee stings to relieve symptoms associated with the disease. Scientific literature has identified melectin, an antimicrobial peptide (AMP) found in bee venom, as a promising agent against *Borrelia burgdorferi*, the bacterium responsible for Lyme disease. AMPs have several advantages over traditional antibiotic treatments. However, extracting melectin directly from bee venom is expensive, labor-intensive, and intrusive to bee populations. Existing synthetic production methods are also limited by cost and scalability, leaving some patients to resort to bee sting therapy.  
To address these challenges, our team is developing improved methods for the synthesis of bee venom-derived AMPs. Our goal is to develop a synthesis and purification workflow that is substantially more accessible and cost-effective than existing approaches. In parallel, we are building computational models to predict AMP behavior directly from protein sequence data. By combining computational modelling with scalable synthesis techniques, we hope to improve access to affordable and effective AMP-based treatments for patients who may not be able to benefit from existing therapies.  
As a student-led research team, we rely entirely on external sponsorships and donations to enable our work. All team members are unpaid, and contributions directly fund laboratory supplies, computational infrastructure, student training, and participation in the international iGEM program. We deeply appreciate any support that enables us to continue conducting and sharing research that may benefit patients in need.

# Novel Melectin Based AMP for treating Lyme Disease

**Problem Statement**

Lyme disease, caused by *Borrelia burgdorferi*, is the most prevalent vector-borne illness in North America, with over 470,000 estimated cases annually in the United States. Despite this significant burden, treatment options remain limited to a small group of oral antibiotics, primarily doxycycline, amoxicillin, and cefuroxime, which were developed decades ago and not specifically optimized for *Borrelia*. Although these regimens are effective in early-stage infection, they have a critical limitation: approximately 10–20% of patients who receive timely, guideline-adherent treatment subsequently develop Post-Treatment Lyme Disease Syndrome (PTLDS), characterized by persistent symptoms such as fatigue, cognitive impairment, and musculoskeletal pain. Outcomes for late-stage disseminated disease are even less predictable, and no approved treatment exists for confirmed antibiotic-refractory cases.

A central mechanistic challenge is *Borrelia*'s exceptional ability to evade the immune system and persist in various phenotypic forms. The pathogen can transition into morphologically distinct states, including dormant "persister" cells and biofilm-like aggregates, which display significantly reduced susceptibility to conventional antibiotics that primarily target actively replicating cells. Broad-spectrum antibiotic regimens also present additional challenges, such as disruption of the host microbiome, increased risk of resistance development, and contraindications in specific patient populations, including pediatric and pregnant patients for whom doxycycline is unsuitable.

Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) constitute a structurally and mechanistically distinct class of therapeutic candidates that may address several of these limitations. AMPs primarily disrupt microbial membranes, a mechanism effective across various bacterial growth states and less susceptible to resistance development compared to target-based antibiotics. Notably, several AMPs have demonstrated activity against *Borrelia* persister forms in vitro, indicating potential efficacy in patient populations unresponsive to current standard-of-care treatments. However, AMP-based therapeutics have not yet been clinically developed for Lyme disease, and significant translational barriers persist, including challenges related to in vivo stability, host cell selectivity, and pharmacokinetic optimization for a pathogen that disseminates extensively into tissue reservoirs. Consequently, there is an unmet clinical need for novel antimicrobial strategies that combine targeted anti-*Borrelia* activity, efficacy against non-replicating persister forms, and a resistance profile that supports long-term therapeutic viability.
