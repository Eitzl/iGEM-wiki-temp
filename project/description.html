Project Description

Biosynthetic production of Melectin — an antimicrobial peptide from Melecta albifrons bee venom — using a duckweed expression system and oleosin oil-body purification.

The Problem: Inaccessible Therapeutics

Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) represent one of the most promising classes of next-generation therapeutics. Unlike conventional antibiotics, AMPs act by physically disrupting microbial membranes — a mechanism that is inherently harder for bacteria to develop resistance against. Among naturally occurring AMPs, Melectin stands out for its potency, selectivity, and multi-indication potential.

There is one significant problem: obtaining Melectin is expensive and non-scalable. The peptide is currently produced either by direct venom extraction from the cleptoparasitic bee Melecta albifrons (a slow, low-yield process requiring specialized handling of live bees) or by solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) (a chemical synthesis method that becomes cost-prohibitive above milligram quantities). As a result, research-grade Melectin costs hundreds of dollars per milligram, placing it entirely out of reach for low-resource labs, field applications, and eventual clinical deployment in low-income settings.

💡

Our value proposition: by expressing Melectin in a cheap, scalable plant chassis and self-purifying it via oil-body flotation, we can reduce the cost of producing research-grade Melectin by an estimated one to two orders of magnitude.

What Is Melectin?

Melectin is an 18-amino-acid cationic antimicrobial peptide isolated from the venom of Melecta albifrons, a cleptoparasitic bee found across Europe and Western Asia. It adopts an amphipathic alpha-helical conformation in membrane-mimicking environments, presenting a positively charged face and a hydrophobic face — the classical architecture of a membrane-active peptide.

Melectin vs. Melittin

Melectin is often compared to Melittin, the major peptide toxin in European honeybee (Apis mellifera) venom. Both are cationic, membrane-active AMPs from bee venoms. The key distinction lies in selectivity:

Melittin (Apis mellifera)

  • 26 amino acids, +6 charge
  • Potent hemolytic activity — lyses red blood cells
  • Disrupts both bacterial and eukaryotic membranes
  • Strong pro-inflammatory; limits therapeutic window
  • Extensively studied but poor therapeutic index

Melectin (Melecta albifrons) ✓

  • 18 amino acids, +4 charge
  • Significantly lower hemolytic activity vs. Melittin
  • Preferentially targets negatively charged membranes
  • Better therapeutic index; lower mammalian toxicity
  • Understudied — presents a genuine research opportunity

Mechanism of Action

Membrane selectivity: why bacteria and not us

The selectivity of Melectin (and AMPs generally) for bacterial over mammalian membranes comes down to membrane charge composition:

  • Bacterial membranes carry a net negative surface charge, dominated by phosphatidylglycerol (PG), cardiolipin, and lipopolysaccharide (in Gram-negatives). Cationic AMPs are electrostatically attracted to these surfaces.
  • Mammalian membranes present a zwitterionic outer leaflet (phosphatidylcholine, sphingomyelin) with near-zero net charge. Cholesterol also stiffens the bilayer, reducing peptide insertion efficiency.

Upon binding, Melectin's amphipathic helix inserts into the hydrophobic core of the bacterial membrane. At sufficient local concentration, this leads to membrane permeabilization via a mechanism tentatively attributed to a toroidal pore or carpet model — [describe specific model based on MD simulation results from dry lab when available]. This results in ion leakage, depolarization, and cell death.

The Expression System: Duckweed

We selected Lemna minor (common duckweed) as our plant chassis. Duckweed is a small free-floating aquatic plant with several properties that make it attractive for recombinant protein production:

1

Rapid growth

Doubling time of ~24–36 hours under optimal conditions (16h light / 8h dark, 25°C, Hoagland's nutrient solution). Biomass can be scaled by expanding surface area of growth vessels — no fermenter required.

2

Established transformation

Stable nuclear transformation via Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated T-DNA delivery is well-documented for Lemna. We use strain EHA105 carrying a binary vector with our oleosin-Melectin fusion cassette.

3

Containment advantages

Aquatic growth in closed, controlled vessels reduces risk of environmental escape compared to soil-grown GMO crops. Duckweed does not survive in most non-aquatic environments and is physically isolated from wild populations.

4

Low input costs

Growth requires only water, light, and a simple nutrient solution. No specialized growth media, CO₂ supplementation, or sterile fermentation required at pilot scale. Well-suited for resource-limited settings.

5

Post-translational processing

As a eukaryote, Lemna can perform disulfide bond formation, glycosylation, and proper folding that prokaryotic systems cannot — important if future iterations require modified or fusion peptide variants.

📝

Why not E. coli? AMPs are often toxic to bacterial expression hosts, causing poor yield or requiring refolding from inclusion bodies. Plant-based expression sidesteps this challenge, and the oleosin system is intrinsically plant-adapted.

Purification Strategy: Oleosin Oil-Body Fusion

The central innovation of our production pipeline is the oleosin fusion tag. This strategy exploits the biology of plant seed oil bodies — lipid droplets coated with oleosin proteins — to create a self-assembling, buoyancy-based purification handle.

How oil-body fusion works

  1. Construct design: The gene of interest (Melectin) is expressed as a fusion protein: [Oleosin] — [Protease site] — [Melectin]. The oleosin domain drives localization to oil body surfaces during seed development.
  2. Oil body assembly: In duckweed fronds expressing the construct, oleosin-Melectin fusion protein self-incorporates into lipid droplets during normal cellular lipid metabolism.
  3. Crude extraction: Biomass is homogenized in buffer. Oil bodies float to the top upon centrifugation (low speed, ~1000 × g), separating from the bulk proteome, cell debris, and contaminants.
  4. Washing: Oil body fractions are washed 2–3× with high-salt or detergent-containing buffer to remove non-specifically adsorbed proteins.
  5. Protease cleavage: Melectin is released from the oleosin anchor by digestion with a site-specific protease (e.g., Factor Xa, TEV protease, or enterokinase) at the engineered linker sequence.
  6. Final separation: A second centrifugation step separates the cleaved Melectin (aqueous phase) from the residual oil body fraction. Optional ion exchange polishing step for high-purity applications.

Precedent and validation

The oleosin fusion platform has been successfully used to produce recombinant insulin (Nykiforuk et al., 2006), hirudin (a thrombin inhibitor), and several vaccine antigens in plant systems. Key metrics from published work show purification yields of 60–85% with 3–5 fold concentration relative to whole-cell extract, achieved without any affinity column step.

✓

The oleosin system is particularly well-suited to AMPs because it keeps the antimicrobial peptide sequestered within the oil body until release — protecting the host plant from the toxic effects of accumulated free Melectin.

Therapeutic Applications

Lyme Disease

Borrelia burgdorferi, the causative agent of Lyme disease, is a Gram-negative spirochete with a negatively charged outer membrane — a direct target for cationic AMPs. Early antibiotic treatment is effective, but persistent/late-stage Lyme presents drug-tolerant biofilm-like forms that are recalcitrant to conventional antibiotics. AMPs are known to penetrate and disrupt biofilms. [Describe planned MIC assays against B. burgdorferi or surrogate strains here.]

Parkinson's Disease

The pathological hallmark of Parkinson's disease is the aggregation of alpha-synuclein (α-syn) into Lewy bodies. Multiple AMPs have been shown to inhibit amyloid fibril formation and disaggregate existing fibrils via amphipathic helix insertion into the hydrophobic core of the aggregate.
